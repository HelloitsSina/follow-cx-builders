# Sample Framework

An example of what a synthesised CX framework looks like after 8–10 weeks of digests.
This is illustrative — yours will be different based on your context and reflections.

---

## My CX Operating Principle

Great CX in my team starts with reliability, not heroics. Before we chase the brilliant interaction, we fix the inconsistent ones. When customers arrive frustrated, we name the emotion before we name the solution. And when we see the same problem three weeks in a row, we stop treating it as a support issue and start asking what's broken upstream.

---

## My Clearest Conviction

**Consistency before WOW.**

Shep Hyken's framing changed how I measure my team. I used to celebrate the outstanding tickets. Now I pay attention to the middle — the random, unremarkable interactions that make up 80% of what we do. If the average is reliably good, the team is doing its job.

---

## The Tension I Haven't Resolved

**Empathy vs. efficiency.**

Chris Voss says label the emotion first, always. Carly Agar says the best interaction is one that never needed to happen — optimise for deflection. These pull in opposite directions. When someone is frustrated and confused, do I invest in the human moment, or do I ask why the confusion happened in the first place and fix it so the next person doesn't need to call?

I think the answer is: both, in sequence. Fix upstream first. But when someone does reach us, be fully present. I haven't figured out how to resource both well simultaneously.

---

## My Blind Spot

**Voice → Action.**

I read the insights from Swinscoe and Quiseng. I agreed with all of them. But I haven't written a single reflection about what I actually do with customer feedback. That probably means I don't have a real answer. VoC is data collection at my company, not a closed loop. I need to sit with this.

---

## My North Star for Hard Decisions

When I don't know what to do, I ask:
> *Will the average customer's experience be more reliable because of this decision?*

Not: "Will it impress anyone?" Not: "Will it fix the one complaint I got this week?" — Does it make the baseline more consistent?

---

## What I'm Still Working On

- Building a real VoC → Action loop (not just collecting feedback)
- Teaching my team the *why* behind policies, not just the *how*
- Understanding where our Un-WOW moments are — I suspect I don't know all of them
- Making the EX → CX connection explicit in how I manage the team
